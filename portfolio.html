    A New Standard for Disaster Recovery Communications

    A first-of-its-kind digital platform guiding disaster survivors through recovery — built under strict constraints, delivered on time.

    Colorado Governor's Office of Climate Preparedness and Disaster Recovery

    Digital platform strategy · User experience · Content design · Deployment planning

    The Need

    People didn't know where to start

    Following the Marshall Wildfire, the Governor's Office recognized that feedback from survivors was consistent: the recovery process felt overwhelming. People didn't know where to start, what they qualified for, or how to move forward. What was needed wasn't just more information — it was a better way to deliver it. The goal became a centralized digital platform that could guide survivors through each stage of recovery and connect them to the right resources.

    The Challenge

    Real constraints, no easy answers

    The office didn't have access to their state-run website or the budget for a full build. As the communications expert brought in with web design experience, I was tasked with finding a solution that met strict constraints: no recurring subscription costs, a three-month development timeline, a tight budget, and the ability to customize for individual disasters as they occur.

    The Solution

     “ AI can't replace human knowledge and expertise, but when used together, it allows for a customized user experience.”

    On the solution to this project

    Finding a Path Forward

    Using AI as a collaborative problem-solving tool

    Given the constraints, I took a different approach. By using AI as a collaborative problem-solving tool, I identified and combined a series of no-cost platforms into a fully integrated website — including a filterable resource database and the ability to customize for future disasters. What initially seemed to require a full digital build was reworked by using existing tools in new ways.

    Balancing AI with Expertise

    AI supported the process — it didn't replace it

    The structure, content framework, and user pathways were built through stakeholder input, user research, and a deep understanding of disaster recovery programs. Once that foundation was established, AI was used to scale and customize content — including organizing large volumes of recovery resources, tagging them for filtering, and developing tailored content across six distinct user pathways.

    My Approach

    User-First Mindset I approach everything with the user in mind — making sure what we create meets both their needs and the organization's goals. When you're working with storm survivors, that matters even more. Access to technology may be limited, and there can be education, language, or accessibility barriers. The experience also needed to be trauma-informed, which shows up in how information is written, structured, and delivered so it feels supportive instead of overwhelming.

    Research and Stakeholders I led stakeholder working sessions to understand user needs, barriers to accessing assistance, how people were finding information, and how those needs shift depending on the type of disaster. Everything pointed to the same issue: people weren't just looking for information — they were trying to figure out what to do next, how to do it, what they qualified for, and how not to accidentally disqualify themselves from aid. There was too much information and no clear starting point.

    User Pathways By defining user types — homeowners, renters, and landlords — I built pathways that guide people step-by-step based on their specific situation. These pathways walk users through each phase of recovery: early, ongoing, and long-term, clearly outlining what needs to happen at each stage. We also made sure the information accounted for real-life factors like caring for elderly family members, individuals with disabilities, or pets, so people can see themselves in the experience and understand what applies to them.

    Content and Navigation Content and navigation were designed to reduce overwhelm and make complex processes feel more manageable. Instead of giving people everything at once, the experience surfaces what they need at the right time. People could navigate by user type, phase of recovery, or resource category. We included "need to know" sections, key takeaways, and printable checklists — so users could keep track of what they needed to do even without consistent access to the platform.

    Deployment Plan In addition to building the platform, I developed a communications plan for how the website would be deployed and promoted before, during, and after a disaster. This included building awareness ahead of disasters, coordinating launch efforts during active events, and sustaining outreach throughout the recovery process — making sure local governments, community organizations, and residents know where to find the tool and how to use it at each stage.

    Phase II — In Development

    Expanding the experience with a custom AI caseworker

    Phase II will add a custom AI tool designed to function as a digital caseworker — allowing users to ask case-specific questions and receive tailored guidance based on their individual situation. It will also incorporate additional multimedia elements including video, graphics, and visual tools to communicate complex information in more accessible and engaging ways.

    The Results

    State partners described the tool and level of information provided as “empowering” — giving people the clarity and confidence to take the next step in their recovery journey.

    State partners on the completed tool

    Developed a first-of-its-kind digital storm recovery navigation tool that provides customized, user-specific guidance to disaster survivors.

    Created a reusable framework that other disaster recovery agencies can adopt — either within existing state websites or adapted on alternative platforms.

    Designed a sustainable, adaptable model that positions Colorado to better support communities in future disasters at scale.
